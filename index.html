Field notes

Where Empathy Meets Execution

Working notes on how human-centred design thinking actually shows up in the day-to-day of running product and programme teams — not as a workshop you run once, but as a default way of seeing the work.

Empathy is a scheduling decision

Most teams say they're user-centred. Few put a real user in the room before the roadmap is locked. The teams I run treat empathy as an operational choice, not a mindset: field visits before sprint planning, apprentice reports read in full, support tickets triaged by the product lead — not just support. Understanding shows up in the calendar before it shows up in the deck.

Prototypes are for arguments, not approval

A prototype's job is to surface disagreement early, while it's still cheap to be wrong. In practice, that means treating early design reviews as a place to find the sharpest objection in the room, not to collect sign-off. If a review ends with everyone nodding, the prototype probably wasn't specific enough to disagree with.

Iteration needs a stopping rule

Rapid iteration without a defined "good enough" just produces busywork with better vocabulary. Every sprint I run pairs the iteration loop with an explicit threshold — a metric, a user reaction, a deadline — so teams know when to stop refining and ship.

Innovation is a distribution problem

Across bootcamps, marketplaces, and county health rollouts, the bottleneck is rarely the idea — it's getting a good idea to the fiftieth apprentice or the thousandth trader in a form they'll actually use. Coordination and design thinking end up being the same discipline in practice: closing the gap between what's possible and what's usable, at scale, on a deadline.
